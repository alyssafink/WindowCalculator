ENERGY STAR Replacement Windows

ENERGY STAR replacement windows are the industry standard for home window energy efficiency upgrades. They come in a wide variety of materials, colors, styles, and operabilities, so you’ll be able to find an option that works for your needs.
They are often the most expensive home window upgrade option, so it can be beneficial to make sure your windows need it before investing.
To help you decide if ENERGY STAR replacement windows are the right window upgrade option for your home, this page will cover the following topics and share some trustworthy resources for more research.

Stuff

There are three key properties that define the impact that windows have on your home. All three window upgrade options selectively modify these properties to help you save money, lower your energy use, increase comfort, and more.
U-Value
Window u-values are a measurement of insulating capability. The lower the number, the better a window is at keeping heat out in the summer and in during the winter.
ENERGY STAR replacement windows have a better u-value than single-pane windows for a few reasons. First, the addition of an insulating air gap between the two panes of glass acts like the thick wall of a cooler to add insulating capability. Second, they typically have one or two low-emissivity (low-e) coatings, which help reflect heat, keeping it out in the summer and in during the winter.
SHGC
The solar heat gain coefficient (SHGC) corresponds to the fraction of solar radiation that passes through a window into your home. The lower the number, the more money you will save on cooling bills. The SHGC of single-pane windows typically ranges from 0.47 - 0.66, depending on whether your windows are already tinted or not. This means that 47-66% of the solar radiation that comes into contact with your current windows is passed into your home.
The coatings included in ENERGY STAR replacement windows are spectrally selective, which means that they let in solar radiation that you want (daylight) and reflect solar radiation that you don’t want (infrared radiation / solar heat).
Visible Transmittance
Visible transmittance is the fraction of sunlight visible to the human eye that passes through a window into your home. The visible transmittance needs of every home are different - some need a lower number to reduce glare, some need to maximize daylighting, and some are somewhere in the middle. The best way to figure out if the visible transmittance of a product is right for your home is to ask the manufacturer or distributor for images of past projects where it has been installed.

Comparing Products
In order to compare different windows to determine which one is best for your home, look for both the ENERGY STAR label for the Southern climate zone and the NFRC label. NFRC-certified products are independently tested, certified, and labeled to help you make the best purchase decision for your needs. While the ENERGY STAR label tells you if a product is energy-efficient, the NFRC label helps you compare between energy-efficient products by breaking down a product’s energy performance based on the properties listed above.

Vinyl windows are typically the least expensive, and wood windows the most expensive. Historically-accurate custom windows can cost $4,000+ each to manufacture and install. Measuring Impact: The Window Calculator analysis considers two aspects of a window upgrade option’s environmental impact: its initial carbon impact and its operational carbon impact. These are measured in kilograms of carbon dioxide equivalent (kg CO2e). A higher number indicates that a product has contributed more to global warming through manufacturing, transportation, installation, energy use, or disposal.
Embodied Carbon: Embodied carbon is a measurement of the greenhouse gas emissions created in order to manufacture an ENERGY STAR replacement window, transport it, and install it in your home. Most of the embodied carbon comes from the production of the glass and primary frame material. How It Works: ENERGY STAR replacement windows cut the amount of sound transmitted through your windows by more than half compared to single-pane windows. This is mostly because of the insulating air gap between the two panes of glass and improved air sealing.
Why Windows Matter: Windows are typically the weak point in your home for sound transmission, letting in the most traffic and airplane noises, along with other annoyances like your neighbor’s leaf blower. This means that storm windows can help save your sanity - and your sleep. If the exterior walls of your home aren’t insulated, though, you’ll need to add insulation in order to really reduce the amount of noise that travels inside.
Sound Ratings: Sound transmission through windows is measured through two different ratings: STC and OITC. STC (or Sound Transmission Class) is a measure of how well a window blocks commons sounds, like conversation. OITC (or Outdoor/Indoor Transmission Class) is a measure of how well a window blocks lower-level noises, like airplanes and construction. For both, a higher number is better. STC ratings are more common to find on windows, but sometimes you will see an OITC rating as well. In either case, it is important to keep in mind that a rating difference of ~3 is nearly impossible for the human ear to discern, while a rating difference of 10 cuts the amount of sound transmitted in half.
If dramatically improving sound insulation is important to you, consider checking out the Sound Blocking page of your personalized home window upgrade report to weigh the benefits of your other window upgrade options.

Windows and Comfort: Your HVAC systems work hard to keep the air temperature comfortable when windows let heat into and out of your home, but windows affect how comfortable your home feels in three other ways, too.
Sunlight: Sunlight contributes to both comfort and discomfort, depending on the season. During the winter, sitting in the sun can feel quite pleasant, even if the air temperature is a bit cooler than you would prefer. During the summer, however, you might want to avoid that sunny window seat.
Glass Temperature: Poorly-insulated windows can get quite hot in the summer, reaching 120 °F or more. Sitting next to poorly-insulated windows can feel like sitting next to a sun-baked brick wall in the summer, making you sweat even if the air temperature is comfortable.
Air Movement: The difference in temperature between your windows and the indoor air can cause drafts if it is extreme, which make you feel colder in the winter. Air leakage through windows also lets in hot air in the summer, and cold air in the winter.
Temperature Control: ENERGY STAR replacement windows make your home more comfortable by controlling the temperature of the glass during extreme weather. The insulating air gap between the two panes of glass lets less heat through, and the low-e coating included between panes reflects heat away, keeping it outside your home in the summer, and inside your home in the winter. Replacement windows without the ENERGY STAR label will feel hotter in the summer and colder in the winter because they aren’t as insulating and let more solar heat through.
Decreased Air Leakage: Because ENERGY STAR replacement windows don’t get as cold in the winter as single-pane windows do, adding them will help reduce any drafts that your home experiences. They are also more air tight than existing windows, especially if they are installed well. In addition to reducing unwanted air leakage, this helps keep out unfiltered air that may contain pollen and other air pollutants.

If you live in a historic home or in one of Tucson’s Historic Preservation Zones, replacing windows can be tricky. Generally, the goal is to maintain the exterior appearance of such homes as much as possible. Historically-accurate replicas of custom wood or steel windows can be expensive or hard to find, and it is often more cost-effective to pursue other window upgrade options unless your existing windows are beyond repair.
Historic Properties: If your home is listed on the National Register of Historic Places, window replacements should be approached with caution. Reach out to the Arizona State Historic Preservation Office to discuss whether replacements that aren’t identical would compromise your eligibility for the State Historic Property Tax Program. They may recommend storm windows as an alternative or only replacing windows that aren’t visible from the street, especially if historically-accurate replicas are unavailable or outside of your budget.
Historic Preservation Zones: If you live in one of Tucson’s Historic Preservation Zones, replacing your windows will require a minor review process involving your local advisory board, the TPCHC Plans Review Subcommittee, city staff, and the PDSD director. Exact requirements vary by neighborhood, but projects that retain the same frame material, window divisions, and operability are most likely to be approved. Aluminum, vinyl, and fiberglass windows are often prohibited. If your windows are beyond repair and historically-accurate replicas are unavailable or outside of your budget, replacement windows may be more likely approved for locations that are not visible from the street. Otherwise, interior-mounted low-e storm windows are a great upgrade option because they can match the color/style/operation of your existing windows and are permanent, but still easily removable.
